Welcome to the eras ... books!

Hello fellow swifties, well, the other night I was completely absorted with some of the songs written by our queen Taylor Swift and I couldn't help thinking of the album I was listening to as a book, like, "If this album was a book, which one would it be?" and I started this thing of naming each Taylor's era as a book merely like a game, but turns out that was a great idea, from there I started to think every one of the songs in a very different way, with a new perspective.


So I thought it'd be so funny to share my veredicts with you, in part it was because I wanted to know if you all agree with my choses and the other part because I thought this would turn into a "One book a month" challenge, with her albums we are able to take this challenge for almost one year... ¿what do we think?

So make the friendship bracelets, take the moment and let's get started!


Debut era as: To kill a mockingbird by Harper Lee


Like Harper Lee's classic, Taylor's debut era captures the essence of small-town life, with its innocence, coming-of-age themes, and poignant storytelling.

The unforgettable novel of a childhood in a sleepy Southern town and the crisis of conscience that rocked it. "To Kill A Mockingbird" became both an instant bestseller and a critical success when it was first published in 1960. It went on to win the Pulitzer Prize in 1961 and was later made into an Academy Award-winning film, also a classic.


Compassionate, dramatic, and deeply moving, "To Kill A Mockingbird" takes readers to the roots of human behavior - to innocence and experience, kindness and cruelty, love and hatred, humor and pathos.

Fearless era as: Pride and Perjudice


Austen's vision of love, growth and society reminds us to Taylor's storytelling all through fearless, she talks of life the way she's experiencing it and the way she percieves it from the experience of those ones close to her. Austen and Swift share a special kind of irony to talk about the world and the love.

Since its immediate success in 1813, Pride and Prejudice has remained one of the most popular novels in the English language. Jane Austen called this brilliant work "her own darling child" and its vivacious heroine, Elizabeth Bennet, "as delightful a creature as ever appeared in print." The romantic clash between the opinionated Elizabeth and her proud beau, Mr. Darcy, is a splendid performance of civilized sparring. And Jane Austen's radiant wit sparkles as her characters dance a delicate quadrille of flirtation and intrigue, making this book the most superb comedy of manners of Regency England.

Speak now era as: Jane Eyre by Charlotte Bronte


As well as Swift's third studio album, Jane Eyre is a masterpiece bout speaking one's truth and defying societal expectations, much like the protagonist of "Jane Eyre." Both stories feature strong-willed heroines who assert their independence and follow their hearts despite the odds.

Charlotte Brontë tells the story of orphaned Jane Eyre, who grows up in the home of her heartless aunt, enduring loneliness and cruelty. This troubled childhood strengthens Jane's natural independence and spirit - which prove necessary when she finds employment as a governess to the young ward of Byronic, brooding Mr Rochester. As her feelings for Rochester develop, Jane gradually uncovers Thornfield Hall's terrible secret, forcing her to make a choice. Should she stay with Rochester and live with the consequences, or follow her convictions - even if it means leaving the man she loves? A novel of intense power and intrigue, Jane Eyre dazzled readers with its passionate depiction of a woman's search for equality and freedom.

Red era as: Wuthering Heights by Emily Bronte


"Red" by Taylor Swift is known for its exploration of intense emotions, passionate relationships, and tumultuous experiences of love and heartbreak. "Wuthering Heights" by Emily Brontë shares many thematic elements with Taylor Swift's "Red" album, making it an apt comparison between intense emotions, turbulent relationships, longing, regret and a dark atmosphere of drama.

At the centre of this novel is the passionate love between Catherine Earnshaw and Heathcliff - recounted with such emotional intensity that a plain tale of the Yorkshire moors acquires the depth and simplicity of ancient tragedy.

1989 era as: The great Gatsby by F. Scott Fitzgerald


Comparing "The Great Gatsby" by F. Scott Fitzgerald to Taylor Swift's album "1989" might initially seem like a stretch, given the stark differences in medium and time period but heir shared exploration of themes such as wealth, identity, love, and the passage of time allows for intriguing parallels to be drawn between them, parties, friends, loneliness and broken hearts are present in both masterpieces.

This exemplary novel of the Jazz Age has been acclaimed by generations of readers. The story of the fabulously wealthy Jay Gatsby and his love for the beautiful Daisy Buchanan, of lavish parties on Long Island at a time when The New York Times noted "gin was the national drink and sex the national obsession," it is an exquisitely crafted tale of America in the 1920s.

Reputation era as: Gone Girl by Gillian Flynn


Overall, "Gone Girl" and Taylor Swift's "Reputation" era share a thematic resonance in their exploration of deception, perception, and the complexities of public image and personal identity. They both share different perspectives on betrayal, love and society standards for a woman, and how they treat her by the way she acts, teh way she talks, teh way she lives. No one wills for a woman to have such a revenge, and when they do, they do it the right way.

Who are you? What have we done to each other?

These are the questions Nick Dunne finds himself asking on the morning of his fifth wedding anniversary when his wife Amy suddenly disappears. The police suspect Nick. Amy's friends reveal that she was afraid of him, that she kept secrets from him. He swears it isn't true. A police examination of his computer shows strange searches. He says they weren't made by him. And then there are the persistent calls on his mobile phone.

So what did happen to Nick's beautiful wife?

Lover era as: Me before you by Jojo Moyes


Overall, "Me Before You" and Taylor Swift's "Lover" era complement each other well, offering nuanced portrayals of love, relationships, and the journey towards self-discovery and personal fulfillment. But what if I tell you this both masterpieces are sad, beautiful and tragic; full of doubt and insecurities?

They had nothing in common until love gave them everything to lose . . .

A Love Story for this generation and perfect for fans of John Green’s The Fault in Our Stars, Me Before You brings to life two people who couldn’t have less in common—a heartbreakingly romantic novel that asks, What do you do when making the person you love happy also means breaking your own heart?

Folklore era as: Normal people by Sally Rooney


Maybe this is my favourite established bond between a book and an album, "folklore" by Taylor Swift and "Normal People" by Sally Rooney share common themes of intimacy, vulnerability, identity, and the complexity of human relationships, making them resonate with audiences who appreciate introspective and emotionally rich storytelling. Complex and intimate, this works are both going to break your heart and make you feel part of the scenario.

At school Connell and Marianne pretend not to know each other. He’s popular and well-adjusted, star of the school soccer team while she is lonely, proud, and intensely private. But when Connell comes to pick his mother up from her housekeeping job at Marianne’s house, a strange and indelible connection grows between the two teenagers - one they are determined to conceal.

A year later, they’re both studying at Trinity College in Dublin. Marianne has found her feet in a new social world while Connell hangs at the sidelines, shy and uncertain. Throughout their years in college, Marianne and Connell circle one another, straying toward other people and possibilities but always magnetically, irresistibly drawn back together. Then, as she veers into self-destruction and he begins to search for meaning elsewhere, each must confront how far they are willing to go to save the other.

Sally Rooney brings her brilliant psychological acuity and perfectly spare prose to a story that explores the subtleties of class, the electricity of first love, and the complex entanglements of family and friendship.

Evermore era as: The secret history by Donna Tartt


Swift's ninth studio album "evermore" delve into introspective storytelling, folklore, and the exploration of complex emotions. "The Secret History" similarly delves into dark themes, intricate storytelling, and the complexities of human nature.

In this book, under the influence of their charismatic classics professor, a group of clever, eccentric misfits at an elite New England college discover a way of thinking and living that is a world away from the humdrum existence of their contemporaries. But when they go beyond the boundaries of normal morality they slip gradually from obsession to corruption and betrayal, and at last—inexorably—into evil.

Midnights era as: Bluets by Maggie Nelson


"Bluets" by Maggie Nelson and Taylor Swift's album "Midnights" might seem like an odd pairing at first, given their different mediums and styles. However, both works delve into themes of introspection, longing, and the complexities of human emotions.

A lyrical, philosophical, and often explicit exploration of personal suffering and the limitations of vision and love, as refracted through the color blue. With Bluets, Maggie Nelson has entered the pantheon of brilliant lyric essayists.

The Tortured poets department era as: The bell jar by Sylvia Plath


Here we are, at the very endo of the list, with my (maybe) favourite author and an album we hadn't even heard. But I bet this can be a good match since we have two or three clues on the different versions of the album announced at the moment. The bell jar is a masterpiece of a woman's journey, self-doubt, self criticism, broken hearts and a pillow full of tears making it something to help bearing the loneliness and the difficult world we all live in.

The Bell Jar chronicles the crack-up of Esther Greenwood: brilliant, beautiful, enormously talented, and successful, but slowly going under—maybe for the last time. Sylvia Plath masterfully draws the reader into Esther's breakdown with such intensity that Esther's insanity becomes completely real and even rational, as probable and accessible an experience as going to the movies. Such deep penetration into the dark and harrowing corners of the psyche is an extraordinary accomplishment and has made The Bell Jar a haunting American classic.
